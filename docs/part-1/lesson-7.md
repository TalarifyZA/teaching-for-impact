## Why this matters

In the previous section, you designed learning activities that support participation and engagement.

This section focuses on how those activities lead to meaningful learning through practice, feedback, and iteration.

Learning does not happen through exposure alone. Participants need:

- opportunities to practise
- feedback to improve
- time to revise and strengthen their work

Without this, even well-designed activities remain theoretical and difficult to apply.

!!! quote "This section helps you move from..."
    *“Did I deliver the activity?”*  
    to:  
    *“Did learners practise, receive feedback, and improve within the activity?”*

## Core concepts

!!! abstract "Practice, feedback, and iteration"
    Learning becomes more robust when learners apply skills in realistic tasks, receive useful feedback, and improve through repeated cycles of attempt, response, and revision.

**Practice**  
Practice should align with outcomes, reflect real use, and include enough support to help learners make progress.

**Feedback**  
Useful feedback is specific, timely, and actionable. It helps learners better understand what is working and what to improve next.

**Iteration and productive challenge**  
Improvement typically happens through cycles of attempt, feedback, and revision. Learners do not need to get everything right on the first try. Productive challenge is part of learning when it is supported and followed by feedback.

## Practical guidance

### Step 1 — Design meaningful practice

For each learning outcome, identify at least one activity where learners practise the relevant skill on a realistic task — not just listen to an explanation or watch a demonstration.

When designing practice activities, consider using a mix of formats:

- **guided practice** — learners work through a task with structured support, such as a worked example or step-by-step prompts
- **independent practice** — learners attempt a task on their own to build confidence and test their understanding
- **collaborative practice** — learners work together in pairs or small groups to solve a problem or complete a task

Check that each practice activity connects directly to at least one of your intended outcomes. If you cannot link a practice activity to an outcome, reconsider whether the activity is needed.

### Step 2 — Plan when and how feedback will happen

Decide in advance where feedback fits within each activity. For each practice activity, identify:

- **when** feedback will happen — for example, after a first draft, at a mid-point check, or at the end of a task
- **what counts as good performance** — write a short description or checklist so both you and the learners know what to look for
- **who provides feedback** — the facilitator, peers, or both — and allow enough time for it in the session plan

### Step 3 — Build in iteration and versioning

Create at least one point in the learning sequence where learners revise their work based on feedback. Plan this into the session schedule, not as an afterthought.

Simple approaches include:

- first attempt → feedback → revised version within the same session
- returning to a task in a later session with fresh perspective
- labelling versions (such as v0, v1, and v2) so learners can see their own progress

### Step 4 — Enable peer learning

Structure opportunities for participants to learn from each other. Peer learning works best when you provide clear guidance on what to do:

- give learners specific questions or criteria to use when reviewing each other's work
- ask learners to explain their approach or reasoning to a partner before or after a task
- set ground rules for feedback — for example, start with what works well, then suggest one specific improvement
- make sure all participants have opportunities to contribute, not only the most vocal

## Example

A facilitator is designing a half-day workshop for researchers who need to learn how to clean and visualise survey data using a spreadsheet tool.

1. **Design meaningful practice:** The facilitator maps the key outcome — "clean and visualise a dataset" — to a hands-on task. Participants first work through a guided practice exercise: cleaning a messy sample dataset using step-by-step prompts. Then they move to independent practice, choosing a visualisation type and building a chart from the cleaned data on their own.
2. **Plan when and how feedback will happen:** The facilitator decides that feedback will happen at two points: after the data cleaning step (a quick facilitator check using a three-item checklist: no blanks, consistent formats, no duplicates) and after the visualisation step (peer feedback). She writes the checklist on a shared slide so learners know what counts as good performance before they start.
3. **Build in iteration and versioning:** After receiving peer feedback on their charts, participants have 15 minutes to revise. They save their original chart as v0 and their revised version as v1. The facilitator closes with a brief group discussion: "What did you change, and why?"
4. **Enable peer learning:** For the chart feedback round, participants swap screens with a partner. The facilitator provides two specific questions to guide the review: "Is the chart type appropriate for this data?" and "Can you identify the main finding within five seconds?" She sets a ground rule: start with one thing that works well, then suggest one specific improvement.
