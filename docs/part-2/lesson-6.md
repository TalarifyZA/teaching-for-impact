Over the last several lessons, you have been building toward this point. You have designed for openness, found and evaluated resources, understood licensing, adapted materials for your context, and — where needed — created new resources from scratch. Your materials work for your training.

Now the question shifts: can someone else use them?

Sharing is not uploading files and hoping for the best. It is the deliberate process of preparing your materials so they are clear enough, documented enough, and accessible enough that another person — in a context you may never see — can pick them up and put them to use.

## Why this matters

There is a gap between materials that work for you and materials that work for others. You know your own materials intimately — the sequencing logic, the unstated context, the way an activity is meant to flow. But that knowledge is invisible to someone encountering your work for the first time.

Materials that are shared without adequate preparation end up unused. Not because they are bad, but because the effort required to understand them is too high. A facilitator browsing a repository needs to quickly understand what a resource is, who it is for, whether it fits their context, and how to use it. If any of those questions require detective work, they will move on.

The goal of this lesson is not perfection. It is to close the gap between "this works for me" and "this is usable by others" — through documentation, licensing, format choices, and platform decisions that make your work genuinely shareable.

## Choosing a license for your materials

In Lesson 3, you learned how to read and interpret licenses on other people's work. Now you need to make the same decision for your own materials: what will you allow others to do with what you have created?

This is a design choice, not a legal formality. The license you choose shapes who can use your materials, how they can use them, and whether your work can grow beyond your own reach.

### How open do you want to be?

The answer depends on your goals:

**If you want maximum reuse and adaptation** — choose **CC BY**. This is the most permissive Creative Commons license. Others can reuse, adapt, remix, and redistribute your work, even commercially, as long as they credit you. This maximises reach and impact.

**If you want adaptations to stay open** — choose **CC BY-SA**. This allows the same freedoms as CC BY, but requires that anyone who adapts your work releases their version under the same license. This builds an ecosystem where improvements remain open.

**If you want to restrict commercial use** — choose **CC BY-NC** or **CC BY-NC-SA**. These prevent commercial use of your materials. Be aware that "commercial" is broadly defined — it can include use within for-profit organisations, even for internal training. This restriction significantly limits who can use your work.

**If you do not want your work modified** — consider carefully before choosing ND (NoDerivatives). For training materials, preventing adaptation undermines the core value of open education. If someone cannot localise your materials, replace examples for their context, or restructure activities for their time constraints, your resource becomes "free to read" but not "open to use."

!!! tip "When in doubt, choose CC BY"
    CC BY is the default choice for materials intended to have the widest possible impact. It removes barriers to reuse while still ensuring you receive credit. Most OER advocates and institutions recommend CC BY as the standard for open educational resources.

If you adapted materials from other sources, remember that their licenses may constrain your choice. A resource you adapted from CC BY-SA material must also be licensed as CC BY-SA (or compatible). Review the licenses of your source materials before finalising your own.

## Preparing materials for others

### Checking readiness

Before sharing, review your materials from the perspective of someone who has never seen them. This is not about polish — it is about usability.

Work through your materials and ask:

- **Can someone follow the instructions without additional explanation?** Read them as if you were delivering the training for the first time. Where would you need to improvise or clarify?
- **Is the purpose of each component clear?** Can a user tell what each activity, section, or resource is for — not just what to do, but why?
- **Are the prerequisites stated?** What do learners need to know, have, or have completed before using this material?
- **Is anything still in your head?** Notes you make during delivery, adjustments you make on the fly, context you provide verbally — if it matters, write it down.

You do not need to make materials complete or perfect. You need to make them clear enough that a competent colleague can use them with reasonable effort.

!!! tip "Ready enough is ready"
    The most common reason materials are never shared is waiting until they feel complete. They will not. Share when materials are clear enough to be useful, and plan to improve them based on how they are actually used. A first version with rough edges is more valuable than a polished version that never leaves your hard drive.

### Adding documentation

Documentation is the context that makes your materials usable by strangers. At minimum, include:

- **Purpose and learning outcomes** — what the materials are designed to achieve
- **Intended audience** — who the materials are for, including assumed background and skill level
- **Instructions for use** — how to use the materials, including sequencing, timing, and any preparation needed
- **Assumptions and requirements** — what tools, access, or prior knowledge is needed
- **License and attribution** — your chosen license and credits for any adapted materials

Place this documentation where users will find it. A README file in a repository, an introductory section in a document, or a metadata page on a platform all work. The key is that someone should not have to open and read every file to understand what the collection contains and how to use it.

## Choosing formats and platforms

Where and how you share materials affects who can access and use them. These are practical decisions, not afterthoughts.

### Format for access

The format you share your materials in directly affects who can use them and how.

**Editable formats** (Word, Markdown, Google Docs, LaTeX source) allow others to adapt your work — which is the whole point of open materials. If you share only a PDF, you are technically allowing adaptation (if the license permits) but practically making it very difficult.

**Accessible formats** work across devices and conditions. Consider your likely users: do they primarily work on laptops with reliable internet, or on mobile phones with limited data? HTML pages are lightweight and work across devices. Large PDFs with embedded media may be impractical in low-bandwidth settings. Video-heavy resources exclude users without streaming capability.

**Structured documents** with proper headings, alt text for images, and consistent formatting support screen readers and assistive technology. This is not extra work for a niche audience — structured documents are easier for *everyone* to navigate.

!!! warning "PDF is not an open format"
    PDF is useful for final, fixed-layout documents, but it is not ideal as a primary sharing format for OER. It is difficult to edit, inaccessible for some users, and does not adapt well to different screen sizes. If you share a PDF, also provide an editable source file.

### Platform for purpose

Where you publish depends on who you want to reach and how you expect materials to be used.

**Institutional repositories** (your university's or organisation's open access platform) provide permanence and discoverability within academic systems. Good for materials you want to be citable.

**Domain repositories** like [Zenodo](https://zenodo.org/) provide DOIs (persistent identifiers) and are designed for research outputs including training materials. Useful when you want a citable, permanent reference.

**Code and content platforms** like [GitHub](https://github.com/) support versioning, collaboration, and community contributions. Well-suited for materials that will evolve over time, especially technical training.

**Open platforms** like [OER Commons](https://www.oercommons.org/) are designed specifically for educational resources, with metadata fields that support discovery by other educators.

**Your own website or blog** gives you full control and immediate accessibility, but lacks the discoverability and permanence of repositories.

There is no single correct choice. You can publish in multiple places — a primary version on GitHub with a citable snapshot on Zenodo, for example. Choose based on where your intended audience is most likely to look.

### Making materials findable

Even well-prepared materials are useless if no one can find them. Help potential users discover your work:

- **Clear, descriptive titles** — "Introduction to Qualitative Data Analysis: Workshop Materials for Community Health Researchers" is findable; "Module 3 Materials" is not
- **Keywords and tags** — use terms your audience would search for, including discipline, level, method, and format
- **A clear summary** — a 2–3 sentence description that tells someone what the materials are, who they are for, and what they cover
- **Context for use** — a note about the setting the materials were designed for, which helps others judge relevance
